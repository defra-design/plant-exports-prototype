Accessibility statement for the {{serviceName}} service

This service is part of the wider GOV.UK website. There’s a separate accessibility statement for the main GOV.UK website. This page only contains information about the {{serviceName}} service.

This service is run by the Animal and Plant Health Agency (APHA), an executive agency sponsored by the Department for Environment, Food & Rural Affairs (Defra), the Welsh Government, and the Scottish Government.

We want as many people as possible to be able to use this service. For example, that means you should be able to:

  • use browser settings or plugins to change colours, contrast levels and fonts
  • use browser settings or other software to zoom in up to 300% without the text spilling off the screen
  • navigate most of the service using just a keyboard
  • navigate most of the service using speech recognition software
  • listen to most of the service using a screen reader (including the most recent versions of JAWS, NVDA and VoiceOver)

We’ve also made the service text as simple as possible to understand.

AbilityNet has advice on making your device easier to use if you have a disability.

  1. How accessible this service is

    We know some parts of this service are not fully accessible, for example:

    • many parts of the service are not easy to navigate using screen reader technology
    • the page layout and the relationship between different parts of the content may be difficult to understand if you’re using assistive technologies
    • we need to improve the colour contrast on many pages so that is easier to view if you have a visual impairment
    • it isn’t clear what the purpose or destination of some links are

  2. What to do if you have difficulty using this service

    If you have difficulty using this service, contact APHA in England, Scotland or Wales, or Department of Agriculture, Environment and Rural Affairs (DAERA) in Northern Ireland.

    The Equality and Human Rights Commission (EHRC) is responsible for enforcing the Public Sector Bodies (Websites and Mobile Applications) (No. 2) Accessibility Regulations 2018 (the 'accessibility regulations').   6. Technical information about this service's accessibility

    Defra and APHA are committed to making their services accessible, in accordance with the Public Sector Bodies (Websites and Mobile Applications) (No. 2) Accessibility Regulations 2018.

  7. Compliance status

    This service is compliant with the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines version 2.1 A standard. The non-accessible parts are listed below.

  8. Non-accessible content

    There is content in the service on this website that is not accessible.

    If you’re on a screen reader:

    • you may find you have difficulties moving between the different numbered pages. This may make it difficult to navigate the part of the service where you review lists of draft and submitted applications. This fails WCAG 1.3.1 A and 4.1.2 A.
    • you may find it hard to understand how the cookies banner reading ‘Tell us whether you accept cookies’ relates to the wider page content. The structure of the page become unclear when the cookies banner is active on a page. This fails WCAG 1.3.1 A.
    • the hierarchy of content on some pages might not be clear as the headings are presented as being of the same level as each other. This means it’s hard to determine the structure of a page and the relation between different bits of content. This affects the main flow of the service as well as the online survey associated with the service. This fails WCAG 1.3.1 A.
    • it may be hard to understand the purpose of some checkboxes which are not grouped together with the question they relate to. Also, some of the checkboxes are grouped together in an unexpected way which can make it hard for you to understand the true relationship between the different pieces of content. This fails WCAG 1.3.1 A.
    • you may not be made aware that a validation error has been shown to you. This is because the error text does not include the word ‘error’. At the same time, you are not given a ‘change’ link so you can fix the issue easily. This fails WCAG 1.3.1 A.

    There are a number of other accessibility problems, for example:

    • you may find your session ends before you have completed your task. You aren’t given the option to extend your session before it expires. In some cases, you may find the submit option is disabled without warning. This is likely to frustrate and confuse, particularly if you need more time to complete a task in the service. This fails WCAG 2.2.1 A.
    • if you’re using voice activation software, particularly Dragon, you may struggle to enter quantity and unit of measurement and also destination countries into the service. This is because these fields are not properly labelled, making it hard for voice software to identify the field and take you there. This fails WCAG 2.5.3 A.
    • if you’re using any type of assistive technology, some fields may not be properly labelled. This means your technology may not be able to locate or identify the function or purpose of that part of the form. This fails WCAG 1.3.1 A and 4.1.2 A.
    • when you load certain pages, your focus may be moved to a particular field on the page rather than to the top of the page itself. This is unexpected behaviour which may be disorientating. This fails WCAG 2.4.3 A.
    • on the destination countries field, it’s unclear what the true purpose and interaction requirement is for this input field. You may not find it easy to understand what is required of you here or what the purpose of the field is. If you’re navigating the service using keyboard only, you may need to perform more key strokes than should really be needed to navigate a feature like this. This fails WCAG 2.4.3 A.
    • there’s no text or alt text description on the ‘crown copyright’ image link in the page footer, this means you may not be able to understand the destination or purpose of this link. This fails WCAG 2.4.4 A.
    • you may find some links where it isn’t clear what the purpose or destination of that link is. This may mean you can’t predict what will happen when you interact with the link. This fails WCAG 2.4.4 A and 2.4.9 A.
    • if you’re using any kind of assistive technology, you may not be able to interact with the form control element that lets you determine if you will automatically accept delegation requests from importers/exporters. This fails WCAG 1.3.1 A, 2.1.1. A and 4.1.2 A.
    • in some parts of the service colour has been used as the sole means of distinguishing different visual elements. There’s not enough colour contrast for everyone with a visual impairment to be able to distinguish between elements on the page. This fails WCAG 1.4.1 A.
    • some links in the service do not have valid destinations. The ‘view cookies’ link does not lead to a valid destination. This fails WCAG 4.1.2 A.

  9. Content that’s not within the scope of the accessibility regulations

    There are also some accessibility problems that fall outside the scope of the accessibility regulations, for example:

    • some parts of the service don’t have the right level of colour contrast to be accessible to all visually impaired users
    • some tables aren’t well laid out and you may find it hard to understand how the structure and content relate to one another
    • some links aren’t well described
    • sometimes links open in a new browser window without warning
    • some abbreviations and acronyms are used without proper explanation
